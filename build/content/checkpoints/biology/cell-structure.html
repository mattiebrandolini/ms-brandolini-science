ğŸŒ± Big Idea

Cells are the building blocks of every living thing. Some cells are simple. Some cells are complex. The complex cells have tiny parts inside called organelles. Each organelle has a specific job â€” like workers in a factory. Understanding WHICH organelles do WHAT is the key to this checkpoint.

Part 1: Two Kinds of Cells

Every living thing is made of cells. But not all cells are the same.

There are two main types: prokaryotic cells and eukaryotic cells.

Here is an easy way to remember the difference:

Pro = No. Prokaryotic cells have NO nucleus. They have NO membrane-bound organelles. Their DNA just floats in the cytoplasm. Bacteria are prokaryotic.

Eu = Do. Eukaryotic cells DO have a nucleus. They DO have membrane-bound organelles. Their DNA is stored safely inside the nucleus. Plants, animals, and fungi are all eukaryotic.

But here is what they share. ALL cells â€” prokaryotic and eukaryotic â€” have three things:

1. A cell membrane â€” a thin barrier that controls what goes in and out.

2. Cytoplasm â€” the jelly-like fluid that fills the cell.

3. DNA â€” the genetic instructions for the cell.

The big difference is organization. A prokaryotic cell is like one big room with everything mixed together. A eukaryotic cell is like a building with separate rooms. Each room has a specific purpose. Those "rooms" are the organelles.

ğŸ”¬ Prokaryotic vs. Eukaryotic Cells

Notice: the prokaryotic cell has NO nucleus â€” DNA floats freely. The eukaryotic cell has a nucleus and organized compartments.

ğŸ“– Key Terms

Prokaryotic â€” a cell with NO nucleus and NO membrane-bound organelles (example: bacteria)

Eukaryotic â€” a cell WITH a nucleus and WITH membrane-bound organelles (example: plant and animal cells)

Organelle â€” a small structure inside a cell that has a specific job (means "little organ")

Part 2: The Command Center â€” The Nucleus

The nucleus is the most important organelle. It is the control center of the cell.

Why? Because the nucleus holds the cell's DNA. DNA contains all the instructions the cell needs to work. When the cell needs to make a protein, the instructions come from the DNA inside the nucleus.

Inside the nucleus, the DNA is usually spread out in a tangled form called chromatin. Think of it like a messy pile of yarn. When the cell is ready to divide, the chromatin coils up tightly into chromosomes â€” like rolling that yarn into neat balls.

The nucleus also has a small, dense area inside it called the nucleolus. This is where the cell builds ribosomes â€” the tiny machines that make proteins. (More on ribosomes in Part 3.)

The nucleus is surrounded by a double membrane called the nuclear envelope. This envelope has tiny pores â€” like doors â€” that let messages and materials move between the nucleus and the rest of the cell.

Everything outside the nucleus but inside the cell membrane is called the cytoplasm. This is where all the other organelles do their work.

ğŸ“– Key Terms

Nucleus â€” the control center of the cell; holds DNA

Chromatin â€” DNA in its spread-out, tangled form

Ribosome â€” a tiny machine that builds proteins (made in the nucleolus)

Part 3: The Protein Delivery System â€” The Endomembrane System

Cells need proteins to do almost everything. Proteins help with digestion. Proteins fight infection. Proteins send signals. Proteins build structure.

But proteins do not just appear. They are built, processed, packaged, and shipped â€” like products in a factory. This factory is called the endomembrane system.

Here is the step-by-step pathway:

1 The Ribosome builds the protein.
The ribosome reads instructions from the nucleus and starts assembling amino acids into a protein chain. Some ribosomes float freely in the cytoplasm. Others attach to the next stop.
2 The Rough ER processes it.
The rough ER is a network of folded membranes covered in ribosomes. That is why it looks "rough." It is like a highway â€” a membrane-enclosed passageway for transporting materials. The protein enters the rough ER, where it gets folded into the correct shape.

ğŸ”¬ Close-Up: Rough Endoplasmic Reticulum

See the small dots on the membrane? Those are ribosomes â€” they make the ER look "rough." The protein chain passes through the membrane into the inside space (lumen).

3 A vesicle carries it to the Golgi.
A small piece of the ER membrane wraps around the finished protein and pinches off. This creates a tiny bubble called a vesicle. The vesicle carries the protein to the next stop.
4 The Golgi Apparatus packages and sorts it.
The Golgi looks like a stack of flat pancakes. It is the packaging center of the cell. The protein arrives, gets modified, gets a "shipping label," and is sorted for its final destination.
5 Another vesicle delivers it.
The Golgi pinches off another vesicle. This vesicle carries the finished protein wherever it needs to go â€” the cell membrane, outside the cell, or to another organelle.

Follow the arrows: proteins move from the ER â†’ through vesicles â†’ to the Golgi â†’ and finally to the cell membrane or lysosomes.

There is also a Smooth ER. It has no ribosomes on it (that is why it is "smooth"). The smooth ER makes lipids (fats) and helps break down toxins.

One more organelle in this system: lysosomes. Lysosomes are the garbage collectors of the cell. They contain digestive enzymes that break down damaged organelles, waste, and foreign invaders.

ğŸ”½ Going Deeper: The Lumen and Cisternae

The inside space of the ER is called the lumen. This is where proteins fold into their correct shape.

The flat stacks of the Golgi are called cisternae. When a protein moves through the Golgi, it passes through the cisternae from one side (the cis face â€” the receiving side) to the other (the trans face â€” the shipping side). Each cisterna adds a different modification.

When the protein is ready to leave the cell, a vesicle carrying it is secreted â€” released from the cell.

Golgi apparatus â€” stacked membranes that package and sort proteins (the "packaging center")

Vesicle â€” a small membrane bubble that carries materials between organelles

Lysosome â€” organelle that breaks down waste and damaged parts (the "garbage collector")

Part 4: The Power Plants â€” Mitochondria and Chloroplasts

Every cell needs energy. Two organelles are responsible for energy: mitochondria and chloroplasts.

Mitochondria: The Engine ğŸ”‹

Mitochondria are like tiny engines. They burn fuel (glucose) and produce energy the cell can use. That energy comes in the form of a molecule called ATP.

The process is called cellular respiration. It uses glucose and oxygen to make ATP. This happens in almost every eukaryotic cell â€” plant AND animal.

Your muscle cells have thousands of mitochondria because muscles need a lot of energy.

Chloroplasts: The Solar Panel â˜€ï¸

Chloroplasts are like solar panels. They capture sunlight and use it to make glucose (sugar). This process is called photosynthesis.

Chloroplasts contain a green pigment called chlorophyll. Chlorophyll is what makes plants green. It absorbs sunlight energy.

Here is the key connection: chloroplasts MAKE the glucose. Mitochondria BURN the glucose. Plants have BOTH. Animals only have mitochondria.

What Makes These Organelles Special ğŸ”¬

Mitochondria and chloroplasts are different from every other organelle. They have:

ğŸ”¹ Their own DNA (separate from the nucleus DNA)

ğŸ”¹ Their own ribosomes

ğŸ”¹ A double membrane (two layers instead of one)

ğŸ”¹ The ability to divide on their own (like bacteria dividing by binary fission)

Why would an organelle have its own DNA and divide on its own? Scientists believe it is because mitochondria and chloroplasts used to be independent bacteria.

ğŸ”½ Going Deeper: The Endosymbiotic Theory

Billions of years ago, a large ancient cell swallowed a smaller bacterium â€” but did not digest it. Instead, the two organisms lived together. The word for this is endosymbiosis â€” "living together within."

The small bacterium that could use oxygen became the mitochondrion. Later, another ancient cell swallowed a photosynthetic bacterium. That became the chloroplast.

The evidence is strong:

ğŸ”¹ Both have circular DNA, just like bacteria.

ğŸ”¹ Both have their own ribosomes, similar to bacterial ribosomes.

ğŸ”¹ Both reproduce by binary fission â€” splitting in two, like bacteria.

ğŸ”¹ Both have a double membrane â€” the inner membrane was the bacterium's original membrane, and the outer membrane came from the host cell.

This is why mitochondria and chloroplasts are the only organelles that carry their own genetic material. They were once free-living organisms.

The ancient host cell swallowed smaller bacteria. The aerobic one became the mitochondrion. The photosynthetic one became the chloroplast.

Mitochondria â€” organelle that converts glucose into ATP energy (the "engine" or "powerhouse")

Chloroplast â€” organelle that captures sunlight to make glucose (the "solar panel") â€” plants only

Part 5: Plant vs. Animal Cells â€” Same Blueprint, Different Extras

Plant cells and animal cells are BOTH eukaryotic. They share most of the same organelles: nucleus, ER, Golgi, ribosomes, mitochondria, lysosomes, vesicles.

But plant cells have three extra structures that animal cells do not:

ğŸŒ¿ 1. Cell Wall

Outside the cell membrane, plant cells have a thick, rigid layer called the cell wall. It is made of a material called cellulose. The cell wall gives the plant cell its square shape and provides structural support. This is why plants can stand upright without bones.

Animal cells have NO cell wall. They only have the flexible cell membrane. This gives animal cells a round shape that can change.

â˜€ï¸ 2. Chloroplasts

Plant cells have chloroplasts for photosynthesis. This is how plants make their own food from sunlight. Animal cells do NOT have chloroplasts. Animals get energy by eating food.

ğŸ’§ 3. Central Vacuole

Plant cells have one very large central vacuole â€” a big water storage tank that can take up most of the cell. It stores water, nutrients, and waste. When the central vacuole is full of water, it pushes against the cell wall and keeps the plant firm. This pressure is called turgor pressure.

When a plant wilts, it is because the central vacuole has lost water.

Animal cells have small vacuoles, but nothing like the large central vacuole in plants.

What about animal cells? Animal cells have centrioles â€” small structures that help organize cell division. Most plant cells do not have centrioles.
