Pedigree Project

Be Mendel for a day. Trace a trait through your family and discover an inheritance pattern — the same way scientists did before they knew DNA existed.

What we're doing and why

Gregor Mendel didn't know about DNA. He didn't know about chromosomes. He didn't know what genes were. He just looked at thousands of pea plants, tracked which traits showed up in which generations, and found a pattern.

We're going to do the same thing — but with your family as the data. You'll pick a trait, list which of your relatives have it, draw a chart called a pedigree, and look for patterns.

Then we'll use a tool called a Punnett square to predict what should be happening if a single gene with a few versions controls the trait. Your prediction will be partly right and partly wrong. That's the point. Real biology is messier than the simple model — and finding where the model breaks is how scientists improve it.

Important: If you can't or don't want to use your own family — that's totally fine. Use Throckmorton's family (below) instead. Just tell Ms. Brandolini before you start. No questions asked.

The trait you'll track

For this project, we are all working with hair color. We're keeping it simple — three buckets:

Brown
Any brown — light, dark, or in between. Includes light brown that some people might call dirty blond.
Red
Any red. Includes auburn, ginger, strawberry, red brown, brown red.
Blond
Pale yellow. Light enough that you wouldn't call it brown.
Unknown
You don't know, can't tell, or never knew them. This is real data — not a failure.

If you're not sure which bucket someone fits into — pick the closest one. There's no wrong answer. We're making approximations the same way Mendel did when he sorted peas as "yellow" or "green."

How to do this

  1. Make a list of your relatives you can confidently say something about. Start with the closest family — yourself, siblings, parents — and work outward as far as you can. Aim for at least 8 people across at least 2 generations.
  2. For each relative, decide their bucket — brown, red, blond, or unknown. If you're not sure, mark unknown. Unknown is real data.
  3. Draw your pedigree on paper, following the symbols above. Put yourself at the bottom, parents above, grandparents above them. Draw squares for males, circles for females. Color or shade based on the bucket.
  4. Find a partner who picked the same trait (we'll do hair color, so that's everyone). You'll compare your two pedigrees and look for patterns together.
  5. Look for patterns. Does the trait skip generations? Does it appear in some sibling groups but not others? What does that tell you about how it might be inherited?
  6. Predict with a Punnett square. We'll learn how to do this in class. Your goal: pick the most complete piece of your pedigree and try to predict what should have happened.

What Morgan noticed

Morgan looked at her completed pedigree and wrote down patterns she saw:

  • Red hair shows up in Mom's side: Grandma Lisa, Mom, and brother Theo all have red.
  • Blond shows up in both sides: Grandpa Robert, Aunt Beth, and Morgan herself.
  • Brown shows up in Dad's side: both grandparents, Dad, Uncle Tom, and sister Lila.
  • The kids in Morgan's generation (Theo, Morgan, Lila) are all different colors — even though they have the same parents.
  • Morgan herself is blond, but neither Mom (red) nor Dad (brown) is blond. Her blond came from somewhere — both parents must be carrying a hidden blond allele.

This is the kind of reasoning you'll do for your own family. Look at who has what, then ask "where could that allele have come from?"

If you can't use your own family: Throckmorton's data

Some people can't use their own family for this assignment, for many reasons. Maybe you don't know your biological relatives. Maybe your family history is private. Maybe the people who could tell you aren't around. Use Throckmorton's family below instead. The work is the same.

How to handle Throckmorton's gaps:
  • Maternal grandma — passed away. Mark unknown with a dashed shape.
  • Paternal grandpa — never met. Mark unknown with a dashed shape.
  • Uncle on Dad's side — never met. Mark unknown with a dashed shape.
  • Cousin Devin — dyes hair. Mark unknown. (Real scientists distinguish between observed and reported data.)
  • Stepdad — biologically unrelated. Don't include on the pedigree at all.
  • Half-brother — biologically related to Mom only. Include him, but only connected to Mom (not Dad).

What you'll turn in

  • Your hand-drawn pedigree (your family or Throckmorton's).
  • A short list of patterns you noticed (3–5 sentences is fine).
  • One Punnett square showing a prediction for one cross in your pedigree (we'll do this part in class together).
  • A short note on what your prediction got right and what it got wrong (2–3 sentences).

You don't need to be right. You need to show your reasoning — the way you got from data to pattern to prediction. That's the science.
